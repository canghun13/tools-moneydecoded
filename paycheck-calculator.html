Paycheck Calculator FAQ

Why is my take-home pay so much less than my salary? ▾
Multiple deductions reduce gross pay to net pay: federal income tax withholding (based on your W-4), FICA taxes (7.65% for Social Security and Medicare), state income taxes (0–13.3% depending on state), and pre-tax benefits like 401(k) contributions and health insurance premiums. A $75,000 salary typically yields $55,000–$58,000 in take-home pay after all deductions.
What are FICA taxes and how are they calculated? ▾
FICA taxes fund Social Security and Medicare. For 2025, you pay 6.2% for Social Security (on wages up to $176,100) and 1.45% for Medicare — a combined 7.65%. Your employer matches this amount. High earners pay an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax on wages above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly). FICA is calculated on gross wages before most pre-tax deductions.
How does my W-4 affect my take-home pay? ▾
Your W-4 tells your employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. Higher withholding means less take-home pay but reduces or eliminates a tax bill in April. Lower withholding means more money per paycheck but may result in a tax bill or underpayment penalty. The goal is to match your actual tax liability as closely as possible throughout the year.
What is the difference between pre-tax and post-tax deductions? ▾
Pre-tax deductions (traditional 401k, health insurance, FSA, HSA) reduce your taxable income. A $500/month 401(k) contribution at 22% marginal rate only reduces take-home pay by $390 — the tax savings cover the rest. Post-tax deductions (Roth 401k, most supplemental insurance) come out after taxes and don't reduce taxable income. Understanding which bucket each deduction falls into helps you see the true after-tax cost.
Which states have no income tax? ▾
Nine states have no state income tax on wages: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. California has the highest top marginal rate at 13.3%. For high earners, the difference between a high-tax state and a no-tax state can be $20,000–$50,000+ per year in take-home pay — an increasingly significant factor in relocation decisions.

Understanding Your Paycheck Deductions

The gap between your gross salary and your net (take-home) pay can be surprisingly large and confusing. A $75,000 annual salary might produce only $55,000–$58,000 in actual take-home pay after federal income tax withholding, FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare), state income taxes, and any pre-tax benefits like 401(k) contributions or health insurance premiums. Understanding each deduction helps you make better financial decisions — from negotiating salary to planning contributions to optimizing your W-4 withholding.

FICA Taxes: Social Security and Medicare

FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) taxes fund Social Security and Medicare. For 2025, employees pay 6.2% of wages for Social Security (on the first $176,100 of earnings) and 1.45% for Medicare — a combined 7.65%. Your employer pays an equal 7.65% match. High earners pay an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax on wages above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married). Unlike income taxes, FICA taxes are calculated on gross wages before pre-tax deductions like 401(k) contributions, with some exceptions.

How Your W-4 Affects Take-Home Pay

The W-4 form tells your employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. The 2020 redesign of the W-4 eliminated allowances and replaced them with a more transparent system. Claiming higher withholding means less take-home pay per paycheck but reduces or eliminates a tax bill in April. Lower withholding means more money per paycheck but may result in a tax bill or underpayment penalty. Getting the withholding right — neither owing much nor receiving a large refund — means you're keeping more of your money working throughout the year rather than giving the IRS an interest-free loan.

Pre-Tax vs. Post-Tax Deductions

Pre-tax deductions (traditional 401k, health insurance premiums, FSA, HSA, commuter benefits) reduce your taxable income, meaning you pay taxes on a smaller base. A $500/month traditional 401(k) contribution at a 22% marginal rate saves $110/month in federal taxes — your take-home pay only drops by $390, not $500. Post-tax deductions (Roth 401k, most supplemental insurance) come out after taxes and don't reduce your taxable income. Understanding which bucket each deduction falls into helps you see the true after-tax cost of benefits and retirement contributions.

State Income Tax Differences

State income tax varies dramatically by location. Nine states have no state income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (on wages), South Dakota, Tennessee (on wages), Texas, Washington (on wages), and Wyoming. California has the highest top marginal rate at 13.3%. Most states fall between 3–6%. For high earners considering relocation, the difference between a high-tax state like California and a no-tax state like Texas can be $20,000–$50,000+ per year in take-home pay — a factor that increasingly influences where high-income workers choose to live.
